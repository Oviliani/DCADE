Its Berlin, 1940, and the city is filled with fear. At the house on 55 Jablonski Strasse, its various occupants try to live under Nazi rule in their different ways: the bullying Hitler loyalists the Persickes, the retired judge Fromm and the unassuming couple Otto and Anna Quangel. Then the Quangels receive the news that their beloved son has been killed fighting in France. Shocked out of their quiet existence, they begin a silent campaign of defiance, and a deadly game of cat and mouse develops between the Quangels and the ambitious Gestapo inspector Escherich. When petty criminals Kluge and Borkhausen also become involved, deception, betrayal and murder ensue, tightening the noose around the Quangels' necks...

Brilliant Complex characters, original idea with factual basis. How could anyone register a protest against something as pervasive and powerful as the Nazi regime? And how would one react when they discovered the unintended consequences of their action on others? 06 December 2013
Living the nightmare... Fallada, with 'Alone In Berlin', produced a masterpiece in Western literature, and I was truly saddened when I read that he died before it was published. The story centres primarily on Otto and Anna Quangel, an ordinary working-class couple in Berlin, who are moved to extraordinary deeds in undermining the Reich when they learn of their son's death at the front. They begin to drop postcards around the city calling for disobedience to the Nazi regime, a crime punishable by death if caught. This simple gesture perplexes the Gestapo, who place Escherich in charge of the case, and so begins a dangerous game of Hide 'n' Seek. But 'Alone In Berlin' is so much more. Its panoramic sweep of life under Hitler encompasses a huge array of characters and their plight- Enno, the gambling womaniser and his long-suffering wife, Eva; Judge Fromm, a retired legal man whose main aim is to serve Justice; Borkhausen, a seedy, manipulative operator; the staunchly pro-Nazi Persicke family; Father Lorenz, the embodiment of goodness; Dr Reichardt, whose stoical kindness sustains his cell mate, and who asks one of the deepest questions I've ever come across: "Would you rather live for an unjust cause than die for a just one?". Indeed, as the reader is swept along from the conquest of France and a time of plenty, to the end of the war when Germany is ruined, the sheer scale of circumstances, plots and sub-plots, and people, one is reminded of Hugo's majestic 'Les Miserables', such is the scope and desperation of the novel. But, as with Hugo, Fallada ends on a note of promise and hope, and shows that even from the depths of human despair and cruelty, human warmth and kindness can shine through. 'Alone In Berlin' is perhaps even more poignant because it was based on a true story, and a healthy supply of facsimilies are to be found at the back of the book, containing Gestapo reports, photos of the perpetrators and copies of their postcards, all of which really bring this tale of hardship and bravery to life. A real eye-opener... 24 December 2012
An important book Before I read this book I'm ashamed to say I didn't know much about the situation ordinary German citizens found themselves in during WWII. This book helped me to understand the fear and oppression they faced. The book meant more to me because it was based on a true story. I did find the book a bit tough to get into at the start because there's quite a few characters but don't let this put you off - once you get to know the characters it's a difficult book to put down! 24 August 2012
